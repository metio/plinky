# How Plinky talks

Plinky is a piano teacher who is delighted you showed up and has nowhere else to be.
Warm, unhurried, never disappointed in you. Every string in `messages/en.json` is that
teacher speaking, so this page is the contract every one of them keeps.

The register is **warm and inviting** — confident, brief, and glad you're here. Not a
mascot, not a manual.

## Invite, don't instruct

The difference that matters most. An instruction tells the reader what to do; an
invitation tells them what's there and trusts them to want it. Same information,
different relationship.

| Instead of | Write |
| --- | --- |
| Play today's challenge, get graded, and share your result grid. | A new piece every day — the same one for everyone. Give it a go, see how it lands, and share your grid if you'd like. |
| Drop a MusicXML file to add it to your library. | Got a score of your own? Drop it in and it plays like any other piece. |
| Set your hand size to personalize the suggested finger positions. | Tell Plinky how far you reach and the finger positions follow your hands. |
| Press Listen to hear the piece, then play along slowly. | Have a listen first, then play along as slowly as you like. |

A chain of imperatives is the tell: *pick a piece, play it, share it*. One verb is an
offer; three in a row is a chore list. When you catch a chain, keep the first verb and
turn the rest into what happens next.

Imperatives are right on **buttons** — `Save`, `Reload`, `Add to my library`. A button
is the reader's own verb, not an order given to them. Leave those alone.

## Joy is brevity, not exclamation marks

The joyful version of a sentence is almost always the shorter one that trusts the
reader. Punctuation can't add delight that the words don't have, and a `!` on a
sentence doing routine work reads as nervous rather than happy.

- Earn the exclamation mark. `Great — I can hear you!` has earned it; something
  worked. `Set your keys!` has not.
- Cut the throat-clearing: `In order to`, `Please note that`, `You can now`, `simply`,
  `just`. If the sentence survives the cut, it was padding.
- One idea per sentence. Three commas usually means two sentences trying to leave.

## Never nag

There are **no streaks in Plinky, ever** — and the copy carries the same promise the
product does. Nothing implies debt, lapse, or a missed day you should feel bad about.
Say what's there; never why the reader was away.

- `All fresh — nothing to review right now.` ✓
- `Pieces resurface on a widening schedule, so what you've learned actually sticks.` ✓
  — explains the system, blames nobody.
- `You haven't practised in 3 days.` ✗ — never, in any form.

Empty states are an opening, not a scolding: `Play a few notes and they'll appear
here.` ✓

## Bad news stays calm and useful

When something breaks, the reader needs to know what happened, that it isn't their
fault, and what still works. No apology theatre, no blame.

- `This device's storage is full or blocked — new progress isn't being saved.` ✓ —
  what happened, plainly, and the stake.
- `Updates can't be installed on this device right now — the app keeps working, and
  reloading later will retry.` ✓ — names what still works.
- `Error: write failed.` ✗ — true and useless.

## Second person, present tense, active voice

Plinky talks to **you**. It rarely talks about itself, and never in the third person
plural — there's no "we" behind the app. `Plinky can listen through the microphone` ✓;
`We've added microphone support` ✗.

Prefer the present: `Every run is graded` over `Every run will be graded`.

## British English

`practise` the verb, `practice` the noun. `favourite`, `colour`, `personalise`.
Existing keys are inconsistent about this; fix them as you touch them.

## Music words are worth learning

Plinky is a piano teacher, so it uses the real word and makes it easy — a beginner who
finishes a piece should have picked up the vocabulary for free. Don't dumb down; don't
assume either.

- `Beams join fast notes into beat groups.` ✓ — real term, plain gloss.
- `Those line things on the notes` ✗.

## Before you add a string

Read it aloud. If it sounds like a form, a manual, or a disappointed parent, it isn't
Plinky yet. Then check:

- Would a person say this out loud to someone at a piano?
- Is there a verb chain that wants to be an invitation?
- Does any word imply the reader owes practice, or fell behind?
- Would cutting it in half lose anything?

Translators: this register is part of the string. See
[TRANSLATING.md](TRANSLATING.md) — match the warmth in your language's own idiom
rather than transliterating the English.
